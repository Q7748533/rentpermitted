Overview

Orlando's STR regulatory landscape is a jurisdiction puzzle. Within city limits, short-term rentals are largely restricted to tourist-commercial zones — you cannot operate an Airbnb in a typical single-family residential neighborhood. In unincorporated Orange County, Osceola County, and Seminole County, rules differ. This tri-county patchwork means your compliance path depends entirely on which jurisdiction your property falls under. Florida's 2011 state law preempts local STR bans enacted after June 2011, adding a legal layer that protects some existing operators. The market fundamentals are exceptional: Orlando draws 74 million visitors annually, theme park demand is year-round, and the median Airbnb occupancy runs 55-61% — well above national average.

License Application — Step by Step

  1. Determine jurisdiction: City of Orlando, Orange County (unincorporated), Osceola County, or Seminole County
  2. Verify zoning: STRs only in tourist-commercial or mixed-use zones within city limits
  3. Apply for City of Orlando STR Permit ($100) or County permit ($63/2yr Orange County)
  4. Register for Florida DBPR Vacation Rental License (state-level, mandatory)
  5. Register for Tourist Development Tax collection (Orange County Comptroller)
  6. Display license numbers on all listings
  7. Renew annually (city) or biennially (county/state)

Taxes

6% Florida state sales tax + 6% Orange County Tourist Development Tax + 0.5% City of Orlando = 12.5% total. Platforms collect.

Key Operating Rules

  • STRs prohibited in most single-family residential zoning districts within city limits
  • Must maintain DBPR state license and local permit
  • Occupancy limits: 2 persons per bedroom + 2 additional
  • Noise ordinance enforced — quiet hours typically 10 PM–7 AM
  • HOA/condo association rules often more restrictive than city — check CC&Rs first
  • World Cup 2026: expect increased enforcement scrutiny during high-profile events

Penalties for Non-Compliance

  • $250–$500/day for city code violations
  • Unlicensed operation: escalating fines + potential listing removal
  • State DBPR violations: up to $5,000 per offense

Is Orlando STR-Friendly?

Orlando is a demand monster with a zoning problem. If you buy in the right zone (tourist-commercial), the numbers work. If you're looking at a residential neighborhood property, it's a non-starter. World Cup 2026 adds a one-time windfall opportunity for compliant operators. For investors, the play is: buy in tourist zones, get fully licensed before June 2026, and ride the demand wave.

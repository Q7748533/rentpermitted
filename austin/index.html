So You Want to Run an Airbnb in Austin. Here's What Actually Matters.

If you're thinking about listing your place on Airbnb or Vrbo in Austin, this is the stuff you can't afford to miss. What permit you need, how much it really costs, how the taxes actually work, and the one date that changes everything: July 2026. Every number here was fact-checked May 2026.

The big picture

If you rent out a residential property in Austin for less than 30 days at a time, you need a license. That goes for a spare bedroom, a guest house, or a whole standalone home. The city runs a three-tier system that decides where and how you can operate. The agency in charge is Austin Development Services Code Compliance.

Listen, July 1, 2026 is the date you can't ignore. After that, Airbnb, Vrbo, and Booking.com have to show your license number on every listing. No license? The city tells the platform to pull your listing. You've got 10 days. This isn't some vague warning. The city already signed a contract with Deckard Technologies to scan for unlicensed properties, and as of April 2026 they've already flagged 2,785 of them. They're not playing.

Which license do you need?

Pick the one that matches your situation. All three types cost the same fee. The difference is in what you're allowed to do.

TypeWho it's forThe key rulesCost (2 years)

Type 2Standalone house you don't live inUp to 2 units per property. Each additional STR on the same site needs to be at least 1,000 feet from the next one. This is the one that trips up investors.$789 new / $338 renewal
Type 3Apartment building or mixed-useThe greater of 1 unit or 10% of total units for apartments, or 25% for mixed-use buildings with commercial space.$789 new / $338 renewal

How to apply

Documents you'll need

  • A copy of the property owner's driver's license or government ID
  • If you're a tenant applying, proof of your lease or rental agreement
  • If someone other than the owner is handling the application, you'll need a notarized Agent Authorization Form
  • Here's some actual good news: as of October 2025, you can skip the Certificate of Occupancy and the insurance paperwork. Two fewer bureaucratic hoops. That's real time saved.

What you'll pay (these fees are non-refundable)

Licenses now last two years instead of one. That change happened in October 2025, so renewals are half as frequent as they used to be.

Where to submit

  1. A brand new online portal launches May 18, 2026. This should make the whole process faster and less painful.
  2. Until then, go through Austin Finance Online (AFO).
  3. Upload all your documents there.
  4. Pay by credit card or e-check.
  5. Shoot a copy of everything to STRLicensing@austintexas.gov.

What you'll pay in taxes

Austin hits short-term rentals with a combined 17% Hotel Occupancy Tax (they call it HOT). Here's the split:

  • 11% goes to the City of Austin
  • 6% goes to the State of Texas

Platforms like Airbnb and Vrbo collect this tax from your guests and send it to the city for you. You don't have to do the math yourself.

But here's what trips people up: even though the platform handles the money, you still have to file a quarterly report with the city. Yes, even if you had zero bookings that quarter. File a zero report anyway. Miss it and it's a compliance violation. Don't let this one slide.

Questions about HOT? Email hotels@austintexas.gov or call 512-974-2590 (press 1).

Rules you actually need to follow

You need a local contact person

If you don't live in the Austin metro area (Travis, Williamson, Hays, Bastrop, or Caldwell County), you have to designate someone who does. This person must be reachable 24/7 and able to show up at the property within two hours if there's an emergency. Not a mailbox service. Not a PO box. An actual person who can physically get there.

Noise rules your guests need to know

  • Between 10 a.m. and 10 p.m.: no sound equipment louder than 75 decibels at the property line
  • Between 10 p.m. and 10 a.m.: no sound equipment audible beyond the property line at all
  • Between 10:30 p.m. and 7 a.m.: no noise or music that your neighbors can hear

Put a printed copy of these rules somewhere guests will actually see them. The city requires it, and it's your best defense if a neighbor complains.

The city tells your neighbors about you

Every time you get or renew a license, Austin mails your contact info to every property within 100 feet. Your neighbors will know you're running a short-term rental. This is automatic and you pay for it as part of the notification fee.

What happens if you skip the license

  • Fines for operating without a license, plus your listing gets pulled from platforms starting July 1, 2026
  • Advertising an unlicensed STR is explicitly illegal under City Ordinance §4-23-34
  • Too many noise complaints or nuisance violations and your license gets revoked

Bottom line: the city is serious about this now. The days of flying under the radar in Austin are ending.

The numbers that tell the real story

  • About 2,750 active STR licenses as of early 2026. That's up 19.6% from a year ago.
  • Roughly 9,300 listings on Airbnb and Vrbo. Around 75% are unlicensed, which means about 7,000 properties are at risk of getting pulled.
  • $11.6 million in hotel occupancy tax collected in FY2025. That's up from $7 million the year before, a 66% jump.
  • 2,785 unlicensed properties identified by the city's scanning software since January 2026. So far: 65 official violation notices sent, 28 citations issued, and 32 new applications filed as a direct result.
  • 94% of all STR complaints involved unlicensed rentals, according to city data.

Sources: Austin Development Services memo (April 2026), KUT/NPR reporting, StaySTRA market data.

What we think happens after July 2026

When platforms start delisting roughly 7,000 unlicensed properties, two things happen at the same time:

  1. Supply shrinks. Overnight, Austin could lose a huge chunk of its Airbnb and Vrbo inventory.
  2. Licensed hosts gain pricing power. Fewer competitors means you can charge more.

The big unknown is how fast the city moves. Austin plans to phase enforcement over six months, starting with properties that have nuisance complaints. A sudden removal of 7,000 listings is unlikely. But even a 30% to 50% contraction rewrites the market for anyone holding a valid license.
